KRX activates circuit breaker as KOSPI plunges 4.91% on tech selling

South Korea's bourse operator activated a circuit breaker Tuesday after the KOSPI plummeted nearly 5%, ending the session down 4.91%, driven by expanded tech sector losses and heavy foreign selling. The Financial Services Commission head warned of excessive leveraged stock investments, and Finance Minister Koo Yun-cheol announced complementary measures targeting single-stock leveraged ETFs. The KOSPI fell to 7,656.31; the KRX also activated a sell-side sidecar earlier in the session as losses accelerated.

So what: The market stress lands three weeks before the July 24 Section 122 tariff expiry, when uncertainty over renewed US trade pressure could compound sentiment.

ROK, US, Japan FMs to meet in Turkey on sidelines of NATO summit

The foreign ministers of South Korea, the United States, and Japan will convene on the sidelines of the NATO summit in Turkey. The trilateral meeting is the first high-level alignment opportunity since Kim Jong Un's June 25 Workers' Party plenum declared DPRK nuclear-armed state status and ordered annual construction of two warships for five years. Agenda is expected to address the DPRK's accelerating naval weapons program and the Kang Kon destroyer commissioning order issued this week.

So what: The trilateral FM meeting will set the coordinated diplomatic posture heading into the July 27 Korean War armistice anniversary — a historically active period for DPRK provocations.

South Korean 'fake news' law takes effect as journalists' groups raise press freedom concerns

South Korea's amendment to the Network Act targeting the spread of false information took effect July 7, with the Prime Minister stating the law will minimize negative effects of misinformation online. Journalists' groups have raised concerns about its impact on press freedom. The Washington Post covered the story, reflecting international scrutiny of media regulation under the Lee Jae-myung administration.

So what: The law's implementation will be watched by US government interlocutors and press freedom organizations as a signal of the Lee administration's democratic governance posture ahead of bilateral engagements.
