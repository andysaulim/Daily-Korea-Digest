KOSPI surges above 8,700 for fourth day on US-Iran deal optimism

Seoul stocks closed up more than 2 percent Tuesday, finishing above 8,700 points for the fourth consecutive session as a US-Iran ceasefire memorandum of understanding eliminated the Hormuz strait risk premium that had weighed on Korean energy import costs. Brent crude fell 11.16% on the day, and the won strengthened 1.15% against the dollar, reflecting simultaneous relief across energy, currency, and equity markets. South Korea has begun direct consultations with Washington and Tehran on Hormuz transit arrangements for Korean-flagged vessels, with the trade ministry separately engaging the EU on steel import rules.

So what: Improved Hormuz access directly affects ROK energy security consultations with the UAE discussed Tuesday and reduces the near-term upward pressure on Korean import prices.

US-Korea

Rival parties agree to 45-day Assembly probe into election ballot shortage

The Democratic Party of Korea and the People Power Party agreed Tuesday to launch a 45-day special parliamentary investigation into the National Election Commission over ballot shortages reported at more than a dozen Seoul polling stations during the June 3 local elections. The PPP will chair the special committee; a plenary vote to authorize the probe is scheduled for Thursday. Prime Minister Kim Min-seok separately vowed to deal sternly with protesters who blocked sports officials from entering a ballot-counting gymnasium, after a standoff that ended when protesters agreed to allow entry.

So what: The parliamentary probe and the Board of Audit and Inspection's parallel consideration of an accounting audit of the National Election Commission will test the Lee Jae-myung administration's management of post-election political tensions ahead of the Thursday Assembly vote.

ROK nuclear submarine roadmap unveiled — War on the Rocks critique published

In late May 2026, ROK Defense Minister Ahn Gyu-back unveiled a Basic Plan for the Development of Nuclear-Powered Submarines at the inaugural meeting of the Future Defense Strategy Committee, chaired by President Lee Jae-myung. A War on the Rocks commentary argues the roadmap is heading in the wrong direction, citing risks to the US-ROK alliance and regional stability. The Lee administration has framed the nuclear submarine program as a pillar of self-reliant defense capability.

So what: The nuclear submarine program is a live policy debate directly affecting US-ROK extended deterrence consultations and Washington's posture on sharing sensitive naval nuclear propulsion technology with Seoul.
